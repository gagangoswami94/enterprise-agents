# Multi-Agent Workflow: Series A Fundraise Preparation

> Prepare for a Series A process in 3 weeks using financial, strategy, legal, and executive agents in parallel.

## The Scenario

**Company:** VerifyAI (the same company from the MVP workflow) — now 18 months post-launch.
**Metrics:** $1.2M ARR, 47 customers, 112% net revenue retention, growing 15% MoM.
**Raise:** $8M Series A to accelerate enterprise GTM and expand to international markets.
**Timeline:** First investor meetings in 3 weeks.

You need: a clean financial model, a sharp narrative, a complete data room, and a founder who doesn't stumble on the tough questions.

## Agent Team

| Agent | Role |
|---|---|
| CFO Advisor | Build the financial model and audit historical financials |
| Strategy Consultant | Sharpen the market narrative and competitive positioning |
| Legal Counsel | Data room readiness and legal housekeeping |
| CEO Coach | Founder prep — narrative, Q&A, and investor meeting performance |

---

## Week 1: Financials + Narrative (parallel tracks)

### Track A — CFO Advisor

#### Step 1: Historical Audit

```
Activate CFO Advisor.

Company: VerifyAI — B2B SaaS, automated background verification.
Preparing for Series A fundraise. First investor meetings in 3 weeks.

Current state of financials:
- Revenue tracking in Stripe (MRR dashboard available)
- Expenses in QuickBooks, mostly manual categorization
- No formal financial model yet — we've been running lean
- Cap table in Carta (clean: 2 founders, 1 angel, standard SAFE notes)

Step 1: Tell me what I need to clean up before showing financials to an investor.
What do institutional Series A investors expect to see in terms of:
1. Historical P&L format (months, cohorts, segments)
2. Bookings vs. revenue recognition (we recognize monthly)
3. Expense categorization (R&D vs. S&M vs. G&A)
4. Key SaaS metrics: ARR, NRR, CAC, LTV, Churn (gross and net)
5. Any red flags in our numbers they will probe immediately

Give me a prioritized list of what to fix in week 1.
```

#### Step 2: Financial Model

```
Activate CFO Advisor.

Now build the 3-year financial model for the Series A deck.

Current metrics:
- $1.2M ARR, 47 customers
- ACV: $25.5K average (range: $8K SMB to $72K enterprise)
- NRR: 112%
- Gross margin: 74%
- CAC: ~$8,200 blended (SDR + marketing)
- LTV:CAC: 3.4x at 18-month payback
- Team: 8 people (2 founders, 3 engineers, 2 sales, 1 CS)
- Burn rate: $95K/month
- Runway: 14 months

Use of proceeds: $8M
- $3.2M: Scale enterprise sales team (4 AEs + 1 SE)
- $2.1M: Product (2 senior engineers, 1 product manager)
- $1.4M: Marketing (demand gen, brand, events)
- $1.3M: Operations and runway buffer

Model assumptions:
- Enterprise motion: 6-month sales cycle, $65K ACV
- SMB motion: self-serve, 30-day trial-to-paid, $9K ACV
- Assume 24-month runway post-close

Deliver:
1. Monthly model: months 1-36 (revenue, headcount, expenses, burn, runway)
2. Three scenarios: base, upside (+30%), downside (-25%)
3. Key milestones: when do we hit $5M ARR, $10M ARR, break-even
4. The 3 assumptions investors will push hardest — and our defensible answer for each
```

### Track B — Strategy Consultant

```
Activate Strategy Consultant.

Building the Series A narrative for VerifyAI — AI-powered background verification.

Context:
- $1.2M ARR, 47 customers, 112% NRR, 15% MoM growth
- Market: $4.2B background verification market, growing 8% annually
- Current players: Checkr ($500M raised), Sterling (public), HireRight (public)
- Our angle: API-first, faster turnaround (2 days vs. 7-10), AI-powered issue detection

Help me build the investor narrative:

1. The market framing — why is NOW the right time for a new entrant in this market?
   (hint: AI + compliance complexity + remote hiring explosion)

2. Why we win — what specific advantages does VerifyAI have that Checkr can't replicate quickly?
   Don't let me say "we're faster and cheaper" — that's not a moat.

3. The 10-year vision — what does VerifyAI look like at scale? What category are we building?

4. The wedge strategy — why start with background verification to build to what bigger thing?

5. Competitive positioning slide — a 2x2 or clear framework that shows where we sit vs. Checkr, Sterling, HireRight. Be honest about where we're weaker.

6. The inevitable objection: "Checkr will just build this." What's the real answer?
```

## Week 2: Data Room + Legal Housekeeping

### Step 4 — Legal Counsel

```
Activate Legal Counsel.

VerifyAI is preparing a Series A data room. First investor NDA requests expected in 10 days.

We need to identify and resolve any legal issues before diligence begins.

Step 1: Data room checklist
What does a Series A investor expect in the data room? Give me the complete list organized by category:
- Corporate (incorporation, cap table, board minutes)
- IP (assignments, open source usage, third-party licenses)
- Customer contracts (MSAs, enterprise agreements, any custom terms)
- Compliance (FCRA compliance for background checks, state regulations, data privacy)
- Employment (offer letters, equity grants, contractor agreements)
- Financials (as covered by CFO Advisor)

Step 2: Red flag scan
Based on a typical early-stage B2B SaaS company in the HR tech space, what are the 5 most common legal issues that blow up Series A diligence?
For each: what it is, why investors care, and what to do if we have it.

Step 3: FCRA compliance
We process background checks. This makes us a consumer reporting agency under the FCRA.
What specific compliance obligations does this create, and what do we need to document before investors see our legal folder?
```

## Week 3: Founder Prep

### Step 5 — CEO Coach

```
Activate CEO Coach.

I'm the CEO of VerifyAI, about to start Series A investor meetings.
Preparing for 20+ meetings over 4-6 weeks. This is my first institutional fundraise.

Part 1: Pitch narrative
My current 30-second answer to "tell me about VerifyAI" is:
"We're an AI-powered background verification platform that helps HR teams run faster, more accurate checks. We integrate with your HRIS and turn a 7-day process into 2 days."

What's wrong with this? Rewrite it to lead with the problem and the market opportunity, not the product.

Part 2: The 10 hardest questions
Give me the 10 questions that will trip me up in partner meetings, along with the framework for answering each one:
- The "why you?" founder question
- The "what happens when Checkr copies you?" question
- The "your CAC seems high" question
- The "112% NRR is good but not great for SaaS" question
- The "what do your best customers say about you?" question
- (and 5 more you expect based on our profile)

Part 3: Meeting mechanics
I tend to over-explain when nervous. Give me 3 specific habits to develop before I walk into my first partner meeting.

Part 4: Post-meeting discipline
What should I do in the 24 hours after each investor meeting to maximize the chance of a second meeting?
```

### Step 6 — CFO Advisor + CEO Coach (final sync)

```
Activate CFO Advisor.

Final check before first investor meeting.

The CEO will be presenting the financial model and will face hard questions.
Identify the 5 numbers in our model that sophisticated Series A investors will probe immediately.

For each number:
- What the investor is really asking when they ask about it
- Our honest answer
- If the answer is weak, how to contextualize it without being defensive

The CEO needs to own these numbers, not just present them.
```

## Key Patterns

**Financials and narrative in parallel** — CFO Advisor and Strategy Consultant run simultaneously in week 1. They don't depend on each other and both need the full 7 days.

**Legal before diligence, not during** — Investors will send NDA requests before formal diligence. Identifying legal issues in week 2 gives you time to fix them before the data room opens.

**CEO Coach is last, not first** — No point coaching the pitch before the narrative and model are locked. The CEO needs to know the real story before practicing the delivery.

**CFO and CEO Coach overlap at the end** — The financial numbers review is done with the CEO in the room. CEOs who can't answer financial questions lose term sheets in partner meetings.

**Honesty over polish** — The Legal Counsel and CFO Advisor are explicitly asked for what's weak, what might blow up diligence, and what assumptions investors will push on. Preparing for the hard questions is the job.
